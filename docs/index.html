library(tidyverse)
library(readr)

food_outbreaks <- read.csv("outbreaks.csv")
food_outbreaks <- food_outbreaks %>%
#filter for the 12 Midwest states we are interested in
filter(State == c("Illinois","Indiana","Iowa","Kansas","Michigan","Minnesota","Missouri","Nebraska","North Dakota","Ohio","South Dakota","Wisconsin")) %>% 
#select only information we are interested in 
select(State, Year, Month, Status, Illnesses, Hospitalizations, 
Fatalities, Species ) %>% 
#filter for incidents with confirmed status 
filter(Status == "Confirmed")
#replace all the n/a entries with a value of 0 
food_outbreaks[is.na(food_outbreaks)] <- 0

#summarize outbreaks_tidy <- outbreaks %>%
food_outbreaks_tidy <- food_outbreaks %>%
  gather(Effect, Total, c("Illnesses", "Hospitalizations", "Fatalities")) 

#scatterplot food_outbreaks_tidy
ggplot(data = food_outbreaks_tidy,
      mapping = aes(x = Year, y = Total, color = State)) +
  geom_point(shape = 1)  + 
  facet_wrap(~Effect) + 
  geom_jitter(width = 1, height = 1) +
  geom_smooth(color = "black")

Food contamination is a serious problem that many in the United States are concerned about but have little information on. In recent years, we have seen stories of both chemical and microbial food contaminations in the media and government notices. However, the question still remains whether food contaminations are increasing or decreasing. Have foodborne outbreaks changed over time and what types of consequences (illnesses, hospitalizations, or fatalities) have these problems led? In our project, we address these questions by examining foodborne outbreaks between 1998 and 2015 in the United States using information provided by the Center of Diseases and Control (CDC). We focus on the twelve Midwest States so that we could compare states with a geographic proximity and similar population size. Furthermore,the level of food safety regulations differs in each state, allowing an examination of food safety variance by state.

In this scatterplot, we visualize the number of individuals affected by confirmed foodborne illnesses between 1998 and 2015. The numbers of fatalities and hospitalization remain fairly low and similar in the Midwest as a whole (there was only one fatality that was confirmed case food poisoning). There is, however, greater variance in the number of illnesses.
